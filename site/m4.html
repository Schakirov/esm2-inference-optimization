Milestone 4 · Variable-length batching & padding

Real proteomes have mixed-length sequences; batching pads each to its batch's longest member, so the GPU burns compute on padding. Over a fixed 256-sequence pool (lengths 32–512 aa), this milestone quantifies that waste and how much a simple length-sorting strategy recovers. Result: naive batching wastes 41–44% of compute on padding; sorting cuts it to 2–8% and lifts real-token throughput up to 1.75× — at a roughly constant padded-token rate.

Batch-planning library & benchmark

  • src/esm2_perf/batching.py — plan_batches() groups sequence indices (naive order or sorted by length) and tallies real vs padded tokens. Pure Python, no torch — so the padding math is unit-tested on the CPU.

What the benchmark says

Key takeaways

  • Length-sorting is a large, free win. At batch 8 it drops padding from 41% to 2% and raises real-token throughput 1.75× (10,476 → 18,382 tok/s) — purely by reordering, no kernel or precision change. Sorted padded_tokens (74,352) is within 2% of the irreducible actual_tokens (72,720).
  • The win is recovered waste, not a faster GPU. Padded tokens/sec is similar across strategies at each batch size (~17.7k naive vs ~18.8k sorted at bs 8). The GPU computes padded tokens at about the same rate either way; sorting just stops feeding it padding.
  • Padding grows with batch size. Bigger batches span a wider length range, so even sorted batches pad more (2% → 8% from bs 8 → 32). Here smaller batches are more padding-efficient.
  • Padded throughput dips slightly at larger batches (sorted 18.8k → 15.4k tok/s): bigger batches more often contain a long sequence, raising the batch max and the O(L²) attention cost per token — the same quadratic effect seen in the baseline.
  • Memory tracks batch size, not strategy (1.42 → 2.01 GB from bs 8 → 32), well within the L4's 24 GB.
Honest scope

Limitations

Read the win in context

Sequence lengths are uniform in [min, max]; real proteomes are not, so the absolute padding fractions are illustrative — the mechanism (sorting removes intra-batch length variance) and the relative win generalize, but don't quote 41% as universal. Sorting also reorders outputs: a production pipeline must gather embeddings back to input order, and sorting assumes a full batch pool, not streaming. Finally, because attention is O(L²), a long sequence's padding costs disproportionately more time than its token share suggests.
