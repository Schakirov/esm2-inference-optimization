Why max relative error is not shown

Hidden states contain near-zero elements; dividing by them inflates relative error to meaningless magnitudes (1e4–1e5) even when absolute error is tiny. It is recorded in the CSV but deliberately not used as a gate. Absolute error, RMS error, and cosine similarity are the trustworthy columns.

What the harness says

Key takeaways

  • The three pooling implementations are interchangeable. ~2e-6 max error in fp32 is pure floating-point summation-order round-off. The per-sequence loop is an independent oracle for the fast paths now and the Triton kernel in Milestone 6.
  • fp16 ≫ bf16 in accuracy here. fp16's 10-bit mantissa tracks fp32 ~6× tighter than bf16's 7-bit mantissa. bf16's advantage is dynamic range, which this well-scaled encoder does not need — so fp16 is the more faithful low-precision choice for embedding fidelity (throughput was equal in Milestone 2).
  • Pooled embeddings are directionally near-identical in both precisions (cosine ≥ 0.9996), even where strict element-wise allclose fails. Averaging over tokens cancels much of the per-element noise.
  • bf16 "fails" the strict gate, and that is fine. pass=False reflects a 1e-2 element-wise threshold, not a meaningful embedding error — documented rather than hidden, which is exactly why cosine similarity is the headline metric.
Honest scope

What this does & doesn't establish

Relative precision, not absolute ground truth

The reference is fp32 eager on this same L4 — not a CPU or alternate device. The harness measures how far low precision drifts from high precision on identical token ids; it is a relative precision check. Inputs are synthetic uniform-random amino-acid sequences, which does not affect the dtype-vs-dtype numerical relationship measured here.
